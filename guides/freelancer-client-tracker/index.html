A simple client tracker from first reply to paid invoice.

Use one small spreadsheet workflow to keep leads, written scope, delivery dates, invoices, payments, and follow-ups visible—without starting with a full CRM.

Keep authoritative records authoritative. A tracker helps you organize work. It does not replace signed agreements, accounting records, bank records, tax records, or professional advice.

Start with minimum useful fields

Track the next decision—not every possible detail.

A useful freelancer client tracker answers six questions: Who needs a reply? What did we agree? What happens next? When is it due? What was invoiced? What is still unpaid?

More columns do not create more control. Start with fields that change your next action. Add complexity only when repeated work proves you need it.

LeadName or company, service, status, next action, follow-up date.
Written scopeDeliverables, exclusions, revisions, timing, fee, currency, payment terms.
ProjectStatus, next milestone, due date, fee, paid amount, remaining balance.
InvoiceInvoice number, sent date, due date, status, payment date, next follow-up.
The operating loop

Six steps for a small freelance pipeline.

01

Capture one consented lead

Record only details you are allowed to keep. Add the service requested, current status, one next action, and one follow-up date.

02

Use a short status vocabulary

Choose a small set such as New, Replied, Discovery, Quoted, Won, Lost, or Paused. Consistent statuses make a weekly scan useful.

03

Write the scope outside chat

Move deliverables, exclusions, revisions, timing, fee, currency, and payment terms into a reviewable written record before delivery begins.

04

Track one next milestone

Keep the current milestone and due date visible. Avoid turning the tracker into a second project-management system.

05

Separate sent from paid

Record invoice sent date, due date, status, payment date, and follow-up. Reconcile payment against your bank or accounting source.

06

Run a ten-minute weekly review

Scan overdue follow-ups, missing written scope, slipping milestones, open invoices, and records that need reconciliation or deletion.

What good looks like

Each row should point to one next action.

Use the tracker as an exception list. A lead without a next action, a project without a milestone, or an invoice without a status is the signal to fix—not a reason to add another dashboard.

When to move beyond a spreadsheet

Upgrade when coordination—not novelty—requires it.

A local spreadsheet can fit one operator with a small client pipeline. Consider a proper CRM, project-management platform, or accounting system when multiple people edit records, permissions matter, automation must be reliable, audit history is required, or authoritative financial workflows are involved.
